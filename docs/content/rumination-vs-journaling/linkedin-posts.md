## Variant A — The counterintuitive hook

The smarter you are, the more dangerous your overthinking becomes.

I spent a while digging into the research on rumination — the psychology of repetitive, self-focused thinking — and one finding stopped me:

More intelligence doesn't protect you from spiraling. It makes the spiral *worse.* More cognitive horsepower builds a more elaborate, more convincing, more airtight theory of why you're stuck.

Here's what the research (Nolen-Hoeksema, Watkins, and Dr. K's excellent breakdown of it) actually shows:

→ Rumination predicts the *onset* of depression, anxiety, and PTSD — measured before the diagnosis.
→ It makes therapy work *worse.* High rumination going into treatment predicts slower recovery and higher relapse.
→ It feels like insight while it destroys your ability to actually solve the problem.

The mechanism is abstraction. Your mind takes specific, solvable situations and compresses them into un-solvable identity conclusions — "I'm behind," "I'm not a leader," "I'm the problem." And you can't take an action *today* that fixes an "I am."

The fix isn't thinking less. It's thinking *concretely.*

Not "why am I like this" → but "what exactly happened, and what's the next step."

That single shift — from abstract to concrete — is the difference between overthinking that traps you and reflection that grows you.

What's one problem you've been analyzing when you should have been taking the next concrete step?

## Variant B — The practical / leadership angle

"Just think it through" is some of the worst advice we give each other.

The research on rumination is clear: thinking *harder* about a problem, from an activated emotional state, reliably makes you more pessimistic, more abstract, and *less* able to solve it.

This matters enormously for anyone who leads, builds, or creates — because our work rewards deep thinking, and it's easy to mistake a spiral for diligence.

Here's the distinction that changes everything, straight from the clinical literature:

𝗕𝗿𝗼𝗼𝗱𝗶𝗻𝗴 → "Why did this happen? What does it say about me?" (Predicts depression.)
𝗥𝗲𝗳𝗹𝗲𝗰𝘁𝗶𝗻𝗴 → "What specifically happened? What can I do next?" (Predicts growth.)

Same behavior. Opposite mode. Opposite outcome.

A protocol I've started applying — it mirrors Rumination-Focused CBT, which outperforms standard CBT for depression:

1. 𝗥𝗲𝗴𝘂𝗹𝗮𝘁𝗲 𝗯𝗲𝗳𝗼𝗿𝗲 𝘆𝗼𝘂 𝘀𝗼𝗹𝘃𝗲. Never make decisions while activated.
2. 𝗚𝗼 𝗰𝗼𝗻𝗰𝗿𝗲𝘁𝗲. Ban "always / never / I am." Name specifics and next actions.
3. 𝗙𝗶𝘅 𝗰𝗼𝗻𝘁𝗲𝘅𝘁, 𝗻𝗼𝘁 𝗰𝗵𝗮𝗿𝗮𝗰𝘁𝗲𝗿. You can't fix an abstraction — but you can always change your environment.
4. 𝗪𝗮𝘁𝗰𝗵 𝘁𝗵𝗲 𝗽𝗲𝘀𝘀𝗶𝗺𝗶𝘀𝗺. Don't quit an action because it won't fix everything. Direction beats magnitude.

You don't have an overthinking problem. You have a mode problem.

Abstract traps you. Concrete frees you.

## Variant C — The personal / vulnerable angle

For years I thought self-awareness was my superpower.

I could explain, in exhausting detail, exactly why I was stuck. I understood my patterns. I had theories. I journaled about all of it.

Turns out that's not insight. That's rumination — and the research says it's one of the strongest predictors of things getting worse, not better.

The uncomfortable truth I had to sit with: the feeling of "I deeply understand my problem" is often the trap itself. Ruminators consistently report the *highest* confidence in their understanding and the *lowest* ability to actually solve anything.

And journaling — the tool I leaned on — turned out to be neutral. Written one way, my journal was just a beautifully organized archive of why I was broken. It amplified the exact mode I was already stuck in.

What actually helped was smaller and less profound than I wanted:

• Calm down *before* trying to solve anything.
• Write in specifics, not identities. "This meeting went badly," not "I'm bad at this."
• Change my context instead of trying to fix myself.
• End every reflection with one concrete next step.

Growth wasn't more thinking. It was a different *kind* of thinking.

If you're someone who "understands your problems really well" and still feels stuck — this might be for you too.
